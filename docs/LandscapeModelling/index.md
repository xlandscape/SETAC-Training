## What is a Landscape?

Landscapes are fundamentally **cultural areas** — shaped by the interplay of natural processes and human activities:

- The **UNESCO World Heritage Convention (1992)** defines cultural landscapes as *"distinct geographical areas uniquely representing the combined work of nature and of man"*.
- The **Council of Europe (2000)** defines a landscape as *"an area, as perceived by people, whose character is the result of the action and interaction of natural and/or human factors"*.

A landscape is therefore much more than an environmental scenario. Habitat conditions emerge from a complex mixture of natural and anthropogenic driving forces, making it challenging — but essential — to separate causes and effects.

---

## The Role of Landscape in Regulatory Risk Assessment

Landscape modelling has become a cross-sectional topic across regulatory guidance documents in Europe, even though no single dedicated "Landscape Guidance" exists yet:

- The **FOCUS Landscape & Mitigation** document (formally noted in 2008) remains the only formal European document directly addressing landscape-scale pesticide risk assessment.
- Landscape-scale aspects appear regularly in EFSA scientific opinions, SETAC workshops (e.g. NTTP, Modelink, MAgPIE, MAD), and numerous regulatory-scientific initiatives.
- ~99 % of EFSA guidance documents address or contain landscape modelling aspects, explicitly or as underlying fundamentals.

## Purpose and Principles of Landscape Modelling

Landscape modelling in environmental risk assessment is:

- **Purpose-driven and problem-oriented** — models are built only as complex as necessary for the question at hand.
- **Tiered** — ranging from simplified representations (e.g., FOCUSsw ditch) to spatially explicit, real-world landscape scenarios using geodata.
- **Mechanistic and integrative** — linking environmental processes (hydrology, fate, transport) with human activities (pesticide use, agricultural management) and population-level effect models.

Key application areas include:

| Area | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Higher-tier (Tier-4) risk assessment | Spatially explicit, population- and ecosystem-level endpoints |
| Recovery modelling | Population recovery potential in real-world stream networks |
| Holistic risk view | Multiple stressors, indirect effects, ecosystem services |
| Risk management | Landscape design, mitigation options, integrated pest management |
| Digital agriculture | Regional risk management, digital twin approaches |

## Landscape Modelling as a Discipline

Landscape modelling is inherently **interdisciplinary**, drawing on:

GIS · Geoinformatics · Remote Sensing · Environmental Fate Modelling · Ecological Modelling · Data Science · Regulation · Communication

A landscape model provides the *"MIRA@Landscape-scale"* framework: a mechanistic, integrative, realistic, and transparent representation of reality — expressed in maps — that is verifiable, plausible, and facilitates risk communication.

---

## Key Messages

- Cultivated landscapes are shaped by environmental conditions, biotic processes, and human activities.
- *Landscape* is a cross-sectional topic in all risk assessment — there is no clean separation between tiers or disciplines.
- Landscape modelling is **purpose-driven, problem-oriented, and of adapted complexity**. There are no universal, all-purpose models.
- The xLandscape framework provides an open, modular platform for spatiotemporally explicit landscape modelling in risk assessment.
